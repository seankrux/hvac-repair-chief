# R-22 vs R-410A vs R-32: What South Florida Homeowners Need to Know

If you've called an HVAC technician about a refrigerant leak and walked away more confused than when you started, you're not alone. Refrigerant terminology—R-22, Freon, Puron, R-410A, R-32—can be baffling. And in South Florida, where your AC runs nearly year-round, the refrigerant in your system has real implications for your wallet.

This guide demystifies the three main refrigerants and explains what each means for Boca Raton homeowners.

## The Three Main Refrigerants Explained

### R-22 (Freon) — The Phase-Out Refrigerant

R-22, commonly called Freon, was the standard AC refrigerant for decades. If your system was installed before 2010, it almost certainly uses R-22.

**The problem:** R-22 was found to deplete the ozone layer. Under EPA regulations, production and import of R-22 was banned as of January 1, 2020. Only recycled and reclaimed R-22 is now available.

**Cost implications for Boca Raton homeowners:**
- R-22 now costs $100–$175 per pound (vs. roughly $10/lb in 2010)
- A typical system holds 6–12 pounds of refrigerant
- A full recharge could cost $600–$2,100+ in refrigerant alone
- Supply will continue to shrink, and prices will continue to rise

If you have an R-22 system with a refrigerant leak, replacement is almost always the financially sound choice.

### R-410A (Puron) — The Current Standard

R-410A replaced R-22 as the industry standard and was used in virtually all residential systems from 2010 through 2025. It does not deplete the ozone layer and operates at higher pressure than R-22 (systems cannot be retrofitted—R-22 and R-410A equipment are not interchangeable).

**Current status:** R-410A is itself being phased down under EPA regulations. New systems using R-410A will not be manufactured after January 1, 2025. However, equipment already in use can continue to be serviced.

**What this means for you:**
- If you have an R-410A system, you can still get it serviced
- R-410A availability will gradually decrease, though the timeline is longer than R-22's
- Pricing is currently stable at $30–$70 per pound

### R-32 — The Next Generation

R-32 is emerging as a leading replacement refrigerant in newer high-efficiency systems. It offers:

- **Lower Global Warming Potential (GWP)** — About 1/3 of R-410A's climate impact
- **Better efficiency** — Systems run more efficiently with R-32
- **Simpler composition** — Pure refrigerant (not a blend), which simplifies recycling

Several major manufacturers have already transitioned new product lines to R-32, and other newer alternatives (R-454B, marketed as Puron Advance, and R-466A) are also entering the market.

## What Should Boca Raton Homeowners Do?

### If you have an R-22 system:
Replace it. There is no good financial argument for continuing to maintain an R-22 system, especially in South Florida where we're running it nearly every day. The refrigerant cost alone on any significant leak typically exceeds $1,000.

### If you have an R-410A system:
Continue to maintain it. R-410A remains widely available and affordable. When the system reaches end-of-life naturally, you'll upgrade to the next-generation refrigerant at that time.

### If you're buying a new system:
Ask about the refrigerant type. Systems using R-454B or R-32 are the most future-proof choices. Avoid buying new equipment that still uses R-410A if you can, as those systems will eventually face similar serviceability issues.

## Can You Retrofit an Older System to Use a Different Refrigerant?

Generally, no. Refrigerants operate at different pressures and have different chemical properties. The compressor, expansion valves, and coils are designed for specific refrigerants. Retrofitting almost always voids warranties and can damage the equipment. The rare exception: some R-22 systems can use "drop-in" substitute refrigerants, but performance is rarely equivalent and we don't recommend this approach.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My AC is leaking refrigerant—how do I know which type it uses?**
A: Check the data plate on the outdoor condenser unit. It will list the refrigerant type. You can also call us with your model and serial number.

**Q: Is R-22 illegal to use?**
A: No. It's illegal to produce or import new R-22, but existing stocks can still be bought, sold, and used for servicing equipment. It's not illegal to run an R-22 system—it's just increasingly expensive.

**Q: What happens when R-410A is fully phased out?**
A: Similar to R-22, existing equipment can still be serviced with reclaimed/recycled refrigerant. But as supply shrinks, costs will rise. The industry has a long runway—this is a gradual transition, not a cliff.

**Q: If I need a refrigerant recharge, will my technician just top it off?**
A: A responsible technician will first find and fix the leak before recharging. Adding refrigerant without fixing a leak is environmentally irresponsible, technically a violation of EPA rules, and a waste of your money—the refrigerant will just leak out again.
