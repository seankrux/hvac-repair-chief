# 7 Red Flags When Hiring an HVAC Contractor in South Florida

South Florida has thousands of HVAC contractors—licensed and unlicensed, skilled and unskilled, honest and predatory. With AC replacements running $5,000–$14,000 and repairs in the hundreds to thousands of dollars, hiring the wrong contractor is an expensive mistake.

Here are the seven most important red flags to watch for.

## Red Flag #1: No Florida License Verification

Florida requires HVAC contractors to hold a state license (CAC — Certified Air Conditioning Contractor) or an equivalent county-registered license. Without it, they can't legally pull permits, and you have no licensing board recourse if something goes wrong.

**How to check:** Visit myfloridalicense.com and search the contractor's name or company. A legitimate Florida HVAC contractor will have a current, active license on file.

**The red flag:** A contractor who can't provide their license number immediately, or who becomes defensive when you ask. Also watch for contractors who claim to be "under" someone else's license—this is known as unlicensed contracting and is illegal in Florida.

## Red Flag #2: No Permit for Major Work

As established in Florida law, system replacements and new installations require permits. A contractor who offers to "skip the permit to save you money" is:

- Potentially unlicensed
- Exposing you to insurance and resale problems
- Avoiding the inspection process that catches poor workmanship

The permit process adds a small cost and slight delay. Any legitimate contractor includes it as a matter of course.

## Red Flag #3: Pressure to Decide Immediately

The high-pressure close is a classic tactic: "This price is only good today." "I have another job to get to, do you want it or not?" "I can't hold this unit for you."

**Reality:** In a competitive market, good contractors want your business—and they'll still have it tomorrow. Artificial urgency is a manipulation tactic. A company confident in their work and pricing doesn't need to pressure you.

Take at least 24 hours to get a second quote for any job over $1,000.

## Red Flag #4: Diagnosis Without Testing

A professional HVAC diagnosis involves testing—measuring refrigerant pressures, voltage and amperage readings, airflow measurements, and thermostat verification. A contractor who walks around for 10 minutes and declares "your compressor is shot" without instrumentation is guessing.

**What proper diagnosis looks like:**
- Technician connects manifold gauges to check refrigerant pressures
- Records voltage and amperage at key components
- Tests capacitors with a multimeter
- Checks airflow and coil condition

Demand an itemized diagnosis report before authorizing major repairs.

## Red Flag #5: Vague or Verbal-Only Quotes

Any quote over $200 should be in writing. Red flags include:

- Verbal quotes only ("I'll send it to you later")
- Quotes that list only a total price with no itemization
- No mention of warranty on parts or labor
- No specification of the exact equipment model being installed

A legitimate quote specifies the equipment model numbers, all included labor, permit fees (if applicable), and the warranty terms. Get competing quotes in writing and compare them line by line.

## Red Flag #6: No Insurance Documentation

HVAC work involves electrical systems, refrigerants, and work at heights (for rooftop or attic-mounted equipment). If a technician is injured on your property and the contractor lacks proper workers' compensation insurance, you could be liable.

**Always request:**
- Proof of general liability insurance
- Proof of workers' compensation insurance
- Confirm coverage is active (call the insurer or request a current certificate)

A legitimate contractor has no problem providing this documentation—it's standard business practice.

## Red Flag #7: Too-Good-To-Be-True Pricing

South Florida HVAC has real material and labor costs. If a quote comes in 30–50% below all other quotes, ask why:

- Is the equipment a lesser-known brand with poor reliability history?
- Will they use used or reconditioned parts?
- Is the contractor cutting corners on installation quality?
- Are they planning to add charges later?

Competitive pricing is good; implausibly low pricing is a warning sign. The lowest bidder often ends up being the most expensive contractor when callbacks, corrections, and premature failures are factored in.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What questions should I ask an HVAC contractor before hiring?**
A: Ask for their license number (verify it), proof of insurance, permit process, exact equipment model being installed, warranty terms, and whether the price includes everything or if there will be additional charges.

**Q: Is it okay to hire an out-of-state HVAC contractor in Florida?**
A: Only if they hold a valid Florida state contractor license. Florida does not honor out-of-state HVAC licenses. An out-of-state contractor must be licensed in Florida to work here legally.

**Q: How do I find reputable HVAC contractors in Boca Raton?**
A: Referrals from neighbors and friends are valuable. Also look for contractors with substantial Google review histories, A+ BBB ratings, and NATE-certified technicians. Verify the license independently regardless of referral source.

**Q: What if an HVAC contractor damages my home?**
A: A licensed, insured contractor's general liability insurance should cover property damage. Without insurance, recovery is difficult and may require civil litigation.
