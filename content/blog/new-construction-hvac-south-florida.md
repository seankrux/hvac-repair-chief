# HVAC for New Construction in South Florida: What Builders Don't Tell You

Buying a new construction home in Boca Raton or Fort Lauderdale seems like a fresh start—everything brand new, no deferred maintenance, no surprises. But when it comes to HVAC, new construction homes in South Florida come with hidden compromises that buyers rarely discover until years later.

Builders optimize for construction cost and code compliance—not for your long-term comfort or utility bills. Here's what they're not telling you.

## The Builder-Grade HVAC Problem

Most production builders in South Florida install the minimum SEER-rated equipment that meets code, from whichever HVAC brand gives them the best volume pricing. This usually means:

- SEER 15–16 equipment when SEER 18–20 is available for modest upside cost
- Standard single-stage compressors when variable-speed provides dramatically better humidity control
- Minimum required duct insulation levels
- Single-zone thermostat control for entire homes

The system passes inspection and feels fine when you move in. But over time, in South Florida's climate, the performance gaps become apparent.

## Common New Construction HVAC Shortcomings

### Undersized or Oversized Systems

Builders often use simplified rules of thumb for sizing (one ton per 500–600 sq ft) rather than proper Manual J calculations. The result:

- **Oversized systems** that short-cycle and fail to dehumidify (the most common problem)
- **Undersized systems** that can't keep up during South Florida's peak summer days

Demand a Manual J load calculation in writing before your closing, or have an independent HVAC company perform one.

### Low-Efficiency Ductwork

New construction ductwork in Florida is typically flex duct, often installed by subcontractors working quickly. Common issues:

- Sagging runs that reduce airflow
- Excess length and turns that increase static pressure
- Joints not properly sealed with mastic (tape alone fails in heat)
- Insufficient insulation around duct runs in hot attic spaces

### Inaccessible Air Handlers

Builders frequently locate air handler units in attic spaces to preserve floor space. While this is code-compliant, attic air handlers run hotter, are harder to service, and create more risk from condensate drain failures (a clogged line means water damage to your ceiling below).

### Builder's One-Year Warranty Trap

Most builder warranties on HVAC are one year—the minimum. After that, you're dealing with the HVAC manufacturer's warranty (parts only) and your own maintenance relationship with an HVAC company. Make sure the manufacturer warranty is properly registered before the builder's warranty expires.

## What You Can Negotiate Before Closing

If you're buying new construction and haven't closed yet, you have leverage:

| Upgrade | Value | Typical Upcharge |
|---------|-------|-----------------|
| Upgrade to 2-stage or variable-speed compressor | High | $500–$1,500 |
| Upgrade to SEER 18+ equipment | High | $1,000–$2,500 |
| Add whole-home dehumidifier | High (for FL) | $1,500–$3,000 |
| Upgrade duct insulation level | Moderate | $500–$1,000 |
| Add UV air purifier | Moderate | $300–$800 |
| Relocate air handler to interior closet | High | $1,000–$2,500 |

Builders often mark up HVAC upgrades significantly. Get independent quotes before accepting their pricing.

## The First-Year Checklist for New Construction

Once you take possession, do these things immediately:

1. **Register the HVAC equipment** with the manufacturer (get model and serial numbers from the unit data plates) — don't miss the 60-90 day window
2. **Schedule an independent HVAC inspection** — have a contractor you choose (not the builder's subcontractor) inspect the installation
3. **Verify the drain line** is properly routed and the float switch is functional
4. **Document everything** — photograph the equipment, record model/serial numbers, and file all paperwork
5. **Set up a maintenance relationship** with an HVAC company so your warranty documentation starts accumulating from day one

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I request a specific HVAC brand in a new construction home?**
A: Yes—but builders may charge for deviating from their standard. Carrier, Trane, Lennox, and Daikin all have strong South Florida dealer networks. The brand matters less than the model tier (efficiency, features, and proper sizing).

**Q: Should I have an independent HVAC inspection before my builder warranty expires?**
A: Absolutely. Schedule an independent inspection at month 10 or 11—before your one-year builder warranty expires. This gives you time to raise any installation issues while the builder is still responsible.

**Q: Does new construction HVAC need to be balanced?**
A: Ideally yes. A professional air balance ensures each room receives the correct amount of conditioned air per the design. Many new homes are never balanced, resulting in hot/cold spots.

**Q: What happens if my new construction HVAC fails in year 2?**
A: The builder's warranty is typically gone. You'd rely on the manufacturer's parts warranty (if registered) and pay labor out of pocket, or through a maintenance plan if you have one.
