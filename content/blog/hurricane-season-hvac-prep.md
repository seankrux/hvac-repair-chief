# Hurricane Season HVAC Prep: Protecting Your System in South Florida

Hurricane season runs June 1 through November 30—and in South Florida, that's not a distant abstraction. Boca Raton and Fort Lauderdale are in one of the most hurricane-vulnerable regions in the United States. For homeowners, that means protecting thousands of dollars in HVAC equipment from wind damage, flooding, flooding, power surges, and flying debris.

Here's your complete hurricane season HVAC preparedness guide.

## Before Hurricane Season Begins (May)

### Schedule a Pre-Season Tune-Up

The absolute worst time for your AC to fail is during and after a major storm, when demand for HVAC techs spikes and temperatures remain brutal. Get ahead of it:

- Professional tune-up to identify any weak components before storm season
- Verify capacitors, contactors, and refrigerant levels are all in good shape
- Confirm drain lines are clear and float switch is functional
- Check electrical connections and service disconnect

### Review Your Insurance Coverage

Most standard homeowner policies cover HVAC systems for storm damage, but the details matter:

- Verify your outdoor condenser unit is covered
- Understand your deductible (many Florida policies have separate hurricane deductibles)
- Consider whether your policy covers temporary cooling (hotel, portable AC) if your system is damaged
- Document your system with photos, model numbers, and purchase receipts

### Consider a Generator or Battery Backup

After a major storm, power outages in South Florida can last days to weeks. A whole-home generator or large battery backup system allows you to run your HVAC (or at least a mini-split) during extended outages. Size matters: a 3-ton central AC system requires a 10–15kW generator at minimum.

## Immediate Pre-Storm Preparation (24-48 Hours Before)

### Protect the Outdoor Condenser Unit

The outdoor condenser is your most vulnerable HVAC component during a storm. Steps to protect it:

1. **Do NOT enclose or wrap it in plastic** — This traps moisture and can damage the unit. Manufacturers explicitly warn against this.
2. **Strap it down if not already secured** — Condenser units should be bolted to a concrete pad. Verify yours is properly anchored.
3. **Clear surrounding debris** — Remove any loose items, lawn furniture, or plant material within 10 feet that could become projectiles.
4. **Trim surrounding vegetation** — Low-hanging branches that could fall on the unit should be trimmed before storm season, not the night before.

### Inside the Home

- Set thermostat to AUTO and fan to AUTO — prevents issues if power fluctuates during the storm
- Make sure your circuit breaker for the AC is accessible
- Know where your electrical panel and AC disconnect are located

### The Day of the Storm

- **Turn off your HVAC system at the thermostat and the breaker** as the storm approaches
- This protects the system from power surges and prevents water ingress during high winds
- Do NOT run the system during a hurricane — the pressure differential can force water into the duct system

## After the Storm: Before Turning Your System Back On

Rushing to restart your HVAC after a hurricane is a common mistake that causes unnecessary damage. Follow this sequence:

### Step 1: Visual Inspection (Before Touching Anything)

- Walk around the exterior and inspect the condenser unit
- Look for visible physical damage, bent fins, displaced refrigerant lines, or flooding
- Check for debris that may have entered the condenser cabinet

### Step 2: Check for Flooding

- If the condenser unit was submerged or in standing water, **do not restart it**
- Flooded units require professional inspection before operation
- Waterlogged electrical components can fail catastrophically and create fire risks

### Step 3: Allow Drying Time

- If the unit was exposed to heavy rain (but not flooding), allow 24 hours to dry before restarting
- Open your electrical panel, verify no breakers tripped during the storm

### Step 4: Professional Inspection for Any Concern

If there's any visible damage, strange noise, or you're unsure—call an HVAC technician before restarting. The cost of a post-storm inspection ($100–$200) is trivial compared to burning out a $2,000 compressor.

## Post-Storm Demand: What to Expect

After a major South Florida hurricane, HVAC technicians are in extreme demand. Plan accordingly:

| Priority | Wait Time |
|----------|-----------|
| Plan members / contract customers | Hours to 1 day |
| Standard service calls | 3–10 days |
| New customers during peak demand | 1–3 weeks |

This is one of the most compelling reasons for a maintenance plan with a local HVAC company—priority service when it matters most.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Should I cover my AC unit during a hurricane?**
A: No. Manufacturers advise against wrapping or enclosing condenser units. The units are designed to withstand rain. Covering them traps moisture and can cause more damage than the storm itself.

**Q: What if my condenser unit blew over during a hurricane?**
A: Do not attempt to right it or restart it yourself. A tipped condenser has almost certainly damaged the compressor and refrigerant lines. Call a licensed HVAC technician for assessment.

**Q: Does homeowner's insurance cover hurricane damage to my HVAC?**
A: Generally yes, but Florida hurricane deductibles are often 2–5% of insured value—potentially thousands of dollars. Review your policy annually before storm season.

**Q: My power was out for a week—is my AC okay after turning power back on?**
A: It should be, provided the unit wasn't flooded or physically damaged. A power surge when utility power is restored can damage electronics. Consider a whole-home surge protector to protect your HVAC investment.
