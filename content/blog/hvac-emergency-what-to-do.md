# HVAC Emergency? Here's What to Do While You Wait for a Technician

It's 2 PM on a Saturday in August. Your air conditioner just stopped working. The thermostat reads 82°F and climbing. In South Florida, this isn't just uncomfortable—for elderly family members, young children, or people with medical conditions, it can become a safety issue within hours.

Here's exactly what to do while you wait for a technician—and how to assess whether you might fix the problem yourself.

## Step 1: Don't Panic—Check These First

Before assuming you need a technician, rule out the most common simple causes:

### Check Your Thermostat
- Is it set to COOL mode (not HEAT or FAN only)?
- Is the temperature setpoint below the current room temperature?
- Are the batteries dead? (Replace and wait 5 minutes)
- Did someone accidentally switch it to an off mode?

### Check Your Circuit Breakers
- Go to your electrical panel and look for the breaker labeled AC, HVAC, or Air Handler
- If any breaker is in the middle position (partially tripped), turn it fully OFF then back ON
- The outdoor unit (condenser/heat pump) typically has its own dedicated breaker—check both
- Also check the outdoor disconnect switch (a metal box near the outdoor unit)—verify it's in the ON position

### Check Your Air Filter
- A severely clogged filter can cause the evaporator coil to freeze, shutting down the system
- Replace the filter if it's badly clogged
- If the system had a frozen coil, turn it to FAN ONLY for 30–60 minutes to defrost, then switch back to COOL

### Check the Drain Line Float Switch
- South Florida drain lines clog with algae regularly
- When the condensate pan fills up, a float switch shuts off the system to prevent overflow
- Look for standing water in the drain pan near your air handler
- If you can see a clogged drain, try clearing it with a wet-vac at the drain outlet (near the exterior of your home)

## Step 2: Keep Your Home Tolerable While You Wait

If the system is truly down and a technician is en route, focus on managing the heat:

### Close Blinds and Curtains
South-facing and west-facing windows admit enormous solar heat. Close all window coverings, especially on sun-exposed sides of the house. This alone can reduce heat gain by 25–40%.

### Use Portable Fans Strategically
- Move fans to create cross-ventilation if outdoor temperature is lower than indoor
- If it's hotter outside (common in South Florida afternoons), keep windows closed and use fans to circulate air without bringing in hot air

### Create a Cool Room
If you have a window AC unit or a portable air conditioner, designate one room as the refuge. Close off that room and keep everyone in it. Hotels in Boca Raton are always another option for extreme situations.

### Manage Body Heat
- Stay on the lowest floor (heat rises)
- Stay hydrated—dehydration risk increases rapidly in heat
- Wear light, moisture-wicking clothing
- Use wet towels on pulse points (wrists, neck) for rapid cooling

### Consider Vulnerable Household Members
For elderly family members, infants, or people with health conditions, proactive steps matter. If the indoor temperature exceeds 90°F, consider relocating them to an air-conditioned space regardless of how long the wait may be.

## Step 3: Provide Useful Information to Your Technician

When you call for service, have this information ready:

| Information Needed | Where to Find It |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| System make and model | Data plate on outdoor unit |
| System age | Data plate (manufacture date) or installation records |
| Symptoms | When it stopped, any sounds or smells before failure |
| Recent service history | Maintenance records |
| Thermostat settings | Screenshot or photo |

The more information you can provide, the more likely the technician arrives with the right parts for the most probable failure.

## Common Emergency Fixes You Can Do Yourself

| Problem | DIY Fix | Skill Level |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| Tripped breaker | Reset breaker | Easy |
| Dead thermostat batteries | Replace batteries | Easy |
| Clogged drain line (wet-vac method) | Vacuum the drain outlet | Easy |
| Clogged air filter | Replace filter | Easy |
| Frozen coil | Switch to fan-only for 30–60 min | Easy |
| Dirty condenser | Rinse with garden hose (system off) | Easy |

**Do not attempt:**
- Electrical work beyond breaker resets
- Refrigerant-related work
- Compressor or motor access
- Anything requiring opening the electrical panel of the unit itself

## When It's a True Emergency

Call for emergency service (which costs more but is worth it) when:

- Indoor temperature exceeds 90°F with vulnerable occupants
- You smell burning from the unit
- You see sparks, smoke, or fire
- There's water actively flooding from the air handler

For burning smells, smoke, or fire—turn the system off at the breaker immediately and call 911 if necessary before calling HVAC.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long can I wait without AC in South Florida heat?**
A: For healthy adults, indoor temperatures up to 85°F are tolerable with fans, hydration, and window management. Above 90°F indoors is dangerous for elderly people, infants, and anyone with cardiovascular or respiratory conditions. Don't wait if vulnerable occupants are present.

**Q: Will resetting my breaker fix my AC?**
A: If the breaker tripped due to a power surge or minor overload, yes. If it trips again shortly after being reset, there's an underlying electrical problem—call a technician and don't keep resetting it.

**Q: Is it safe to run AC in fan-only mode when something is wrong?**
A: Fan-only mode doesn't activate the compressor or refrigerant system. It's safe to run in most cases and helps circulate air while you wait. It does use electricity and won't cool your home.
