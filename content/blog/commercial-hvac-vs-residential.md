# Commercial vs. Residential HVAC in Boca Raton: Key Differences

If you own or manage commercial property in Boca Raton—a retail space, office suite, restaurant, or multi-tenant building—you already know that keeping the AC running isn't optional. But do you know how commercial HVAC differs from the residential systems most technicians are trained on?

The differences are significant. Hiring a residential HVAC contractor for a commercial system—or vice versa—can mean improper sizing, missed code requirements, and equipment that fails prematurely.

## Scale and Capacity

The most obvious difference is sheer size:

| Property Type | Typical Cooling Capacity | System Type |
|---------------|--------------------------|-------------|
| Single-family home | 2–5 tons | Split system |
| Small office (1,000 sq ft) | 3–5 tons | Split or packaged |
| Medium office/retail (5,000 sq ft) | 15–25 tons | Packaged rooftop |
| Restaurant | 10–30 tons | Multiple zones |
| Multi-tenant commercial building | 50–200+ tons | Chiller or multiple RTU |

Commercial systems are rated in tons and BTUs, just like residential—but they may serve dozens of zones, multiple floors, and widely varying occupancy loads.

## System Types: Commercial vs. Residential

### Rooftop Package Units (RTU)

The workhorse of commercial HVAC in South Florida, rooftop packaged units house the entire system—compressor, evaporator, condenser—in a single cabinet mounted on the roof. Benefits:

- Frees interior floor space
- Easier service access (techs work on the roof, not in occupied space)
- Scalable: buildings simply add more RTUs as needed
- Common in strip malls, offices, and light commercial throughout Boca Raton

### Chilled Water Systems

Large commercial buildings use centralized chiller plants to produce chilled water, which is then circulated through air handling units throughout the building. These systems serve hundreds of tons of cooling load efficiently but require specialized maintenance expertise.

### Variable Refrigerant Flow (VRF/VRV)

VRF systems, common in multi-story commercial buildings, use refrigerant piping (rather than ducts or water) to serve multiple indoor units from one outdoor unit. High efficiency, excellent individual zone control, and complex installation.

## Zoning and Controls Complexity

Residential HVAC typically uses one or two thermostats. Commercial HVAC uses sophisticated Building Management Systems (BMS) or Building Automation Systems (BAS) that integrate:

- Zone-by-zone temperature control
- Occupancy scheduling
- Demand-controlled ventilation
- Energy monitoring and fault detection
- Remote monitoring and control

Understanding and maintaining these control systems requires technicians with commercial HVAC certification and training beyond standard residential credentials.

## Ventilation Requirements

Commercial buildings have much stricter ventilation requirements than residential properties. ASHRAE Standard 62.1 governs commercial ventilation rates based on occupancy type, square footage, and use. Restaurants, gyms, medical offices, and retail all have different required air changes per hour.

Inadequate commercial ventilation doesn't just mean stuffiness—it's a code violation that can result in permit problems, health complaints, and liability exposure.

## Maintenance Differences

Commercial HVAC maintenance is more complex and more frequent:

| Maintenance Task | Residential | Commercial |
|-----------------|-------------|------------|
| Filter changes | Every 30–90 days | Weekly to monthly |
| Coil inspection | Annually | Quarterly |
| Refrigerant check | Annually | Quarterly or more |
| Controls calibration | Annually | Monthly |
| Log keeping required | No | Often yes (EPA, local codes) |

Restaurants face additional challenges: grease-laden air from kitchen exhaust accelerates coil fouling and creates fire hazard risks in nearby equipment.

## Regulatory Differences in Florida

Commercial HVAC work in Florida requires:

- Licensed HVAC contractor (same license as residential, but scope of work matters)
- Commercial building permits (more complex than residential)
- Compliance with Florida Building Code commercial standards
- Compliance with local Boca Raton/Palm Beach County mechanical codes
- Potential LEED or energy code compliance documentation for new construction

## Choosing the Right Commercial HVAC Contractor

Not all HVAC companies are equipped for commercial work. When evaluating contractors for commercial properties in Boca Raton:

- Ask specifically about commercial project experience
- Request references from similar property types
- Verify they have experience with your control system brand
- Ensure they can provide 24/7 emergency response for commercial clients

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can a residential HVAC contractor work on my small commercial space?**
A: Technically yes, if licensed—but experience matters. Small commercial spaces like single-tenant offices often use systems similar to residential, but restaurants, medical offices, and multi-zone buildings require different expertise.

**Q: How much does commercial HVAC maintenance cost in Boca Raton?**
A: Commercial maintenance contracts are priced per-unit (each rooftop unit or air handler) and typically range from $300–$800 per unit per year, with quarterly service visits.

**Q: What's the typical life of a commercial rooftop unit in South Florida?**
A: 12–18 years with proper maintenance. South Florida's heat and humidity accelerate wear compared to northern climates, but well-maintained commercial equipment can exceed this.

**Q: Do commercial HVAC systems use different refrigerants?**
A: Most commercial systems in South Florida use R-410A (now transitioning to R-454B and similar alternatives), the same as residential. Larger chiller systems use different refrigerants (R-134a, R-123, etc.) specific to those system types.
