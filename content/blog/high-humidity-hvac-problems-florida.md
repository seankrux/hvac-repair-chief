# How South Florida Humidity Destroys HVAC Systems (And How to Stop It)

South Florida averages relative humidity of 74% year-round—and during summer months, it regularly spikes above 85–90%. That moisture isn't just uncomfortable; it's quietly destroying HVAC components, encouraging mold growth, and making your home feel warmer than the thermostat reads.

If you've ever walked into a 76-degree home that felt like 82, humidity is almost certainly the culprit. Here's what's happening to your system—and what you can do about it.

## How Humidity Damages HVAC Components

### Evaporator Coil Corrosion

Your indoor evaporator coil runs cold, which means it constantly pulls moisture from the air. In South Florida, that means the coil is perpetually wet. Without regular cleaning, algae, mold, and mineral deposits accumulate—reducing efficiency and eventually causing coil corrosion and refrigerant leaks.

### Drain Pan and Line Clogs

The condensate pan catches moisture removed from your air. In high-humidity environments, algae growth in the drain pan and drain line is almost inevitable without regular treatment. A blocked drain causes:

- System shutoff (float switch triggers)
- Water damage to ceilings, walls, and flooring
- Mold growth in the air handler cabinet

### Short-Cycling and Inadequate Dehumidification

Here's a counterintuitive problem: **oversized AC systems** are a major source of humidity problems. A too-large unit cools your home quickly, then shuts off—before it has time to properly dehumidify the air. You end up with a house that's technically the right temperature but feels clammy and uncomfortable.

The fix is proper system sizing (Manual J calculation) and sometimes a variable-speed air handler, which runs longer at lower capacity—the ideal mode for dehumidification.

### Mold in Ductwork

Humid air infiltrating poorly sealed ducts, combined with the cool interior of duct runs, creates ideal mold conditions. If you notice musty smells from vents, mold colonization in your ductwork may already be underway.

## Measuring Your Home's Humidity

The ideal indoor relative humidity in South Florida is **50–55%**. You can monitor this with an inexpensive digital hygrometer ($15–$30 at any hardware store). If your reading is consistently above 60%, your HVAC system is not adequately dehumidifying.

| Indoor RH | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Below 50% | Excellent |
| 50–55% | Good — ideal South Florida range |
| 55–60% | Acceptable with caution |
| 60–65% | Humidity problem — investigate |
| Above 65% | Serious issue — mold risk |

## Solutions for High Humidity in South Florida Homes

### 1. Ensure Proper AC Sizing

If your system is oversized, it will always struggle with dehumidification. This is a structural problem that no amount of maintenance can fix. A Manual J calculation confirms whether your system is correctly sized.

### 2. Install a Whole-Home Dehumidifier

For homes with persistent humidity problems, a dedicated whole-home dehumidifier (installed in-line with your HVAC system) is the most effective solution. These units:

- Remove 50–130 pints of moisture per day
- Operate independently of the AC cycle
- Are controlled by a humidistat for precision
- Cost $1,500–$3,000 installed

### 3. Switch to a Variable-Speed Air Handler

Variable-speed air handlers run at 30–60% capacity most of the time, circulating air continuously rather than blasting at full power. This dramatically improves dehumidification and provides more even temperatures throughout the home.

### 4. Regular Maintenance

- Clean evaporator coil annually
- Treat drain pan monthly with algae tablets
- Replace filters every 30–60 days
- Seal duct leaks to prevent humid attic air from entering the system

### 5. Seal Your Home's Envelope

HVAC systems can't overcome a leaky building. Weatherstripping, caulking around windows and doors, and attic air sealing all reduce the humidity load on your system.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why does my house feel humid even when the AC is running?**
A: Most often this is due to an oversized system that short-cycles, a refrigerant issue reducing cooling capacity, a dirty evaporator coil, or duct leaks bringing humid attic air into the conditioned space.

**Q: Can high humidity damage my furniture and electronics?**
A: Yes. Sustained humidity above 60% causes wood warping, electronics corrosion, leather deterioration, and book damage. It's not just a comfort issue.

**Q: How much does a whole-home dehumidifier cost in South Florida?**
A: Installed cost typically ranges from $1,500 to $3,000 depending on unit capacity and installation complexity. Aprilaire and Santa Fe are leading brands used in South Florida homes.

**Q: Does running the fan continuously help with humidity?**
A: Generally no. Running the fan without cooling recirculates moisture that has collected in the drain pan back into the air. Use AUTO fan mode, not ON.
